# Latest Briefing: Lance B

**Source:** Stop Trading in "No Man's Land" If You Want to Be Profitable (2026-02-28)

## Key Points
- "No man's land" = price action stuck mid-range with contracting volatility — looks tradable but has negative expected value; most paper-cut losses accumulate here
- Two common traps: (1) stock makes a leg + counter-leg then chops in contraction, (2) consolidation after breakout where traders prematurely position for the next move
- Paper-cut losses compound psychologically — they shrink risk tolerance and cause traders to skip high-EV setups when they actually arrive
- Real example from S&B Capital: trader shorted Nicola twice in no man's land ($2,500 loss), then skipped the actual breakdown — total impact ~$5K (loss + missed profit)
- Exceptions: fresh breaking news invalidates prior range; clear S/R within the range can be traded at reduced size

## Notable Claims or Positions
- 90%+ of paper-cut losses and P&L churning among less experienced traders comes from mid-range trades, not breakout moves
- "Stop obsessing over the jack-five. Focus on the pocket pairs." — mastering marginal setups is stepping over dollars to pick up pennies

## Actionable Takeaways
- Audit trade data: isolate P&L from mid-range trades vs. new-high/new-low trades to quantify how much no man's land costs
- Create rules: max 2 small-size attempts in a range; no shorts after a big down leg unless 30+ min consolidation then new intraday lows
- Size lighter in ranges; save full conviction sizing for breakouts to new prices
